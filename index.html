What is in the Universe?

The universe is filled with a vast array of celestial bodies, ranging from the smallest particles to the most massive cosmic structures. At the core of galaxies, supermassive black holes exert immense gravitational forces, while neutron stars and pulsars remain as dense remnants of massive stellar explosions. The universe is also home to billions of galaxies, each containing countless stars, planets, and nebulae—vast clouds of gas and dust where new stars are born. Dark matter and dark energy, though invisible, make up most of the universe, influencing its expansion and structure. Quasars, extremely bright and energetic galactic centers powered by black holes, shine across cosmic distances. Cosmic microwave background radiation, a remnant of the Big Bang, pervades space as evidence of the universe’s origin. Beyond the observable universe, the cosmos may contain structures and phenomena yet unknown, shaping the grand and mysterious expanse of existence.

Black Hole

Black holes are among the most mysterious cosmic objects, much studied but not fully understood. These objects aren’t really holes. They’re huge concentrations of matter packed into very tiny spaces. A black hole is so dense that gravity just beneath its surface, the event horizon, is strong enough that nothing – not even light – can escape.
The event horizon isn’t a surface like Earth’s or even the Sun’s. It’s a boundary that contains all the matter that makes up the black hole.
There is much we don’t know about black holes, like what matter looks like inside their event horizons. However, there is a lot that scientists do know about black holes.

The universe is everything. It includes all of space, and all the matter and energy that space contains. It even includes time itself and, of course, it includes you. Where do you think you are in this vast universe?

Falling into a Black Hole

Falling into a black hole would look like descending into total darkness, with extreme light distortion around the edges. As you cross the event horizon—the point of no return—you’d see only blackness. The most striking effect would be your body stretching into a thin strand due to intense gravity, a process called spaghettification.

First Image of a Black Hole

Using the Event Horizon Telescope, scientists obtained the first image of the black hole at the center of the galaxy M87. (There is a supermassive black hole at the center of our galaxy — the Milky Way.)
